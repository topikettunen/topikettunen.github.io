Chapter 0 - (when cloud-native lisp)

I have always enjoyed writing, although I wouldn’t call my self a great writer. Without trying to sound too much of a cliche, writing has always been very therapeutic for me and when I can combine it with various topics related to my real-life, it’s a pretty fun! A while ago I had a pretty good habit of blogging various tech-related topics semi-regularly, but unfortunately, I lost that habit so that’s why I’m trying to resurrect that with this blog.

I’m a life long programmer (see Lisp Weenie), musician and a rock climber. Lately, I’ve been working on as a DevOps consultant here in Helsinki, Finland, which I’ve been thoroughly enjoying. In this job I’ve seen how the cloud-native approach for computing and programming is the future but requires discipline and ability of a certain degree so that can be achieved in which I’m trying to help people.

At the same time, I thoroughly love Lisp and I strongly believe it fits to modern cloud-native architectures very well, despite being “old” language. Which is also where the name of this blog comes from, “sky” as in “cloud”. That is also why the main bulk posts in this blog will be relating to how you can utilize Lisp in the cloud in combination with tools such as Kubernetes.

Why Lisp then? Mainly for the reason of my interest and enthusiasm towards the language. I’m not working with Lisp on my day job, apart from some odd jobs in Clojure and ClojureScript, so this is truly a passion project. Robert C. Martin (Uncle Bob) recently made a great statement about Lisp:

Indeed, it’s the language that refuses to die. We’ve tried to kill it many times. But like the annoying neighborhood stray cat, it keeps coming back.

So if Lisp is here to stay, why should we fight against it? Instead, we should use it in a modern environment and if there are limitations we should attack those instead. Uncle Bob also stated greatly that new languages come and go and quite often they don’t bring anything new to the table. Usually, little bit syntactic sugar here and there or some features from some already existing language. Very rarely there comes a new language that strikes my interest, currently, Go, Elixir and Rust being the sole exceptions.

I do understand that it’s quite far-fetched that Common Lisp would see its second coming, but I don’t mind that since I’m doing this mainly for my personal interest. In the same time, there is already quite a big Lisp community, which I don’t see very “modern” on many fronts, so if my incoherent nonsense can spark some interest towards Lisp or Lisp in a modern environment from some readers, I would consider this a success.

My main focus will lie in how Common Lisp can be utilized in a cloud-native environment, although I might post a little bit about other Lisps also (especially Clojure and ClojureScript) here and there. Alongside that, I will write about my various side-projects related to these topics, some smaller, some quite ambitious.

What kind of topics would you be interested in? Leave a comment or feel free to contact me directly!
